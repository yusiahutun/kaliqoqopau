    Washington Uni in St. Louis lecturer says race 'has implications for how we practice medicine'

    A medical school lecturer has warned students in St. Louis that medicine 'has a really bad racism issue', warning those in her seminar that they should not challenge her point of view or else she would 'shut that s*** down real fast'.

    A medical school lecturer has warned students in St. Louis that medicine 'has a really bad racism issue', warning those in her seminar that they should not challenge her point of view or else she would 'shut that s*** down real fast'.

    Kaytlin Reedy-Rogier is named on Washington University in St. Louis's website as a co-lead, community engagement, in the Understanding Systemic Racism Team.

    In a lecture obtained by Fox News, which they said was delivered recently, Reedy-Rogier tells a class of medical students their profession is inherently racist.

    'We are very firm in race being a social construct, and that that has implications for how we practice medicine,' Reedy-Rogier said during the lecture.

    'That has implications for how we understand research, and that has implications for how we understand health disparity.

    'And so when we are asking you all to engage in this, we're really asking you to think about your own identities, and what that means to be anti-racist, which is an active stance in medicine that we know has a really bad racism issue.'

    Reedy-Rogier delivered her lecture to students as part of the compulsory Gateway Curriculum

    In a confusing warning to students, she said she was 'always' open to hearing an opposing point of view, but then told them she would not tolerate dissent.

    'I have a really hard time being neutral around issues of systemic oppression,' she said.

    'So oftentimes you will know how I feel.

    'This does not mean that I am opposed to hearing other perspectives. I would like to be very clear about that: I am always willing to engage in dialogue with folks that may disagree with me. Always.

    'And I will not think less of you, nor will I try to fight you or debate you.

    'And in fact, if you try to fight me or debate me, I will shut that s*** down real fast.'

    Brookings Hall, one of the symbols of Washington University in St. Louis. The medical school has its own Understanding Systemic Racism Team

    The lecture is part of Washington University's 'Health Equity & Justice' lessons, Fox reported, which is included in the medical school's required 'Gateway Curriculum' that was rolled out in 2020.

    In April this year, one of Reedy-Rogier's colleagues, Jessica Pitman, tweeted: 'Why I love being in #MedEd @WUSTLmed - huge turnout for bias reduction & management talk for Phase 1 #GatewayCurriculum students as part of the Health Equity & Justice thread, led by @a_cool_woman & Kaytlin Reedy-Rogier.'

    Pitman added: 'Incredible insight & dialogue! #antiracism @WashUMedODEI'.

    The faculty appears to put a strong emphasis on 'woke' training.

    On Thursday the medical department held a meeting to discuss their 'Commitment to Anti-Racism Statement'.

    In October, they celebrated the training of 21 new 'equity champions'.

    'The Equity Champion role is a vital part of our campus-wide initiative for culture change to understand and dismantle systemic racism,' they said.

    And in September, their 'equity champions' met to discuss ending racism in medicine.

    'We explored how the academic medicine community is addressing and eradicating systemic racism in health care and at @WUSTLmed,' they said.

    Dr. Stanley Goldfarb, the board director of Do No Harm - a group of medical professionals that work to 'protect healthcare from a radical, divisive, and discriminatory ideology' - told Fox that he felt the work was 'undermining' the legacy of the school.

    'Washington University of St. Louis is one of the outstanding research institutions in the country,' he said.

    'Yet it is undermining that legacy with a racist approach to medical education.'

    Goldfarb said the rhetoric was the 'corruption of healthcare', and insisted there was no evidence that doctors treated people differently depending.

    'These videos of faculty teaching medical students that they must consider race as primary factor in practicing medicine is a corruption of health care,' he said.

    'There is no valid evidence that health care disparities are the result of the manner in which patients are treated by physicians.

    'To claim that is the case only prevents an effective approach to reducing disparities such as improving access to health care and better patient and community education about adherence to treatment plans and early recognition of the signs and symptoms of disease.'

    Training in 'anti racism' is spreading throughout medical schools, and 23 of America's 25 most prestigious medical schools have some type of mandatory student training on CRT according to CriticalRace.org.

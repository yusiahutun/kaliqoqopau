    Between the 1970s and 1990s, most of the carry-on music folks listened to hits on cassette tapes.

    At the time, digital music was unheard of, which meant that one had to literally rewind or forward the cassette to the point at which his or her favourite song was. Many people used biro pens or sticks to physically rewind or forward.

    Sometimes the system 'chewed' the brown strand, rendering the particular spot unplayable.

    Each tape had side A and B, carrying as many as 10 songs on each side, with music albums released under the name of volumes.

    The man behind that nostalgic era, 94-year old Dutchman Lou Ottens, has died.

    As at the time of his death, over 100 billion tapes had been manufactured and sold around the world.

    The family is yet to reveal the circumstances surrounding the demise, nor plans for his funeral and burial, but he leaves behind a legacy that will remain etched in the annals of entertainment.

    Cassette tapes made it easy to move around with one's favourite music, listened to either on a walkman or car stereo.

    Millennials may not know what the cassette tape is, having been born in an era with digital music, but those who lived on mixtapes will appreciate that Otten's invention revolutionised the world of music.

    That invention, together with its creator, will now forever remain confined to the books of history.

    Maidstone United's run to the FA Cup fourth round hasn't just been the work of this season - but rather the culmination of 32 years.

    The National League South side had to fight back from the depths of non-league after they were forced out of the EFL in 1992, with the original club going bankrupt.

    They'd only just reached the Football League for the first time in their 95-year history three years prior. But it was the money they spent in doing so which ultimately led to their downfall.

    Their financial problems could largely be attributed to the decision to sell their London Road ground in 1988 to furniture chain MFI. Maidstone moved into a groundshare with Dartford 20 miles away, which saw the average attendance plummet from 2,400 to 1,400.

    Despite their shrinking home support, manager John Still got them promoted. But it was the off-the-field affairs which were the issue, with the owners taking a gamble which didn't pay off when they spent £400,000 on land for a new 10,000-seat stadium.

    The local council refused to grant planning permission and the club's debt started to mount. They finished the 1991/92 season 18th in the old Fourth Division, comfortably clear of relegation.

    But that was to be their final campaign. As well as the club, the entire team was put on the market that summer in a last-gasp attempt to rescue the financial situation.

    By the time they were due to play their first game of the 92/93 season away to Scunthorpe United, only two players - defender Gary Stebbing and striker Glen Donegal - remained, while the Watling Street stadium they'd been using had also been sold.

    Without a squad or a home, the fixture was cancelled and days later they resigned from the league and were liquidated. Former player Warren Barton, who'd departed two years before, gave an insight into just how poorly ran the club was.

    He told The Guardian: “When I got sold for £300,000, which was a considerable amount of money at the time, one of the owners immediately bought himself a brand new Jag. I thought it was a little odd that the first thing they did with the money went into a nice car.”

    Rising from the ashes, a phoenix club was formed around the youth squad, Maidstone Invicta. Initially former owner Jim Thompson took charge, but he was banned from football for his part in their demise.

    Paul Bowden-Brown took over as chairman, a position he held until 2010. During that time they rose through the ranks from the basement of the English footballing pyramid.

    The sale of future Manchester United star Chris Smalling was a major boost, and in 2012 Maidstone finally returned to the town as their Gallagher Stadium home was opened.

    Before relegation in 2022, they spent seven years back in the fifth tier. But this season's FA Cup run is without a doubt the pinnacle of their second existence.

    Standing in their way of reaching the fifth round is Championship high-flyers Ipswich Town. Their clash will be live on BBC One, with kick-off 12.30pm today (January 27).
